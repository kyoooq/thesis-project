INTRODUCTION 
 
Gender mainstreaming has emerged as a central principle in promoting 
inclusivity achieving fair outcomes within academic and institutional settings. This 
involves a critical examination of how knowledge production itself can either make or 
mitigate gender-based inequalities. Consequently, there is a global push for academic 
outputs to demonstrate genuine gender responsiveness and actively contribute to the 
goals of Gender and Development (GAD). Institutions are increasingly held 
accountable to ensure their research output aligns with national and international 
commitments to gender equality, making the accurate evaluation of academic content 
a crucial task. Gender mainstreaming ensures that the government pursues equality 
in development processes and aims for a responsive society where women and men 
equally contribute (Philippine Commission of Women, n.d.). In the Philippines, the 
standard bearer for this evaluation is the Harmonized Gender and Development 
Guidelines (HGDG) (Philippine Commission on Women, 2016). The HGDG serves as 
the official, comprehensive framework designed to assess the gender responsiveness 
of government projects, policies, and, critically, research outputs.  
 
 
Despite the presence of these frameworks, recent studies reveal persistent 
challenges in evaluating gender inclusivity across research disciplines. Palma (2025) 
and Sicuan (2024) observed that while higher education institutions have adopted 
GAD programs, the assessment of research outputs remains inconsistent due to 
limited awareness, varying evaluator interpretations, and the lack of systematic tools. 
Similarly, Llorens et al. (2021) and Brück (2023) demonstrated that gender bias 
continues to shape authorship, citation practices, and publication visibility, showing 
that inequities persist even in supposedly objective academic environments. These 
findings highlight that gender bias in academic writing is not only a social issue but 
also a structural one—embedded in how knowledge is written, cited, and evaluated. 
Fortunately, recent advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Natural 
Language Processing (NLP) offer a proven solution for objective textual analysis. 
Researchers such as Mousavian et al. (2025) have shown how machine learning 
models can identify gendered language patterns and promote fairness in 
computational systems. Recent research, such as Huy Quoc To et al. (2020), shows 
that machine learning techniques such as SVM and Naïve Bayes can be used. 
Meanwhile, Liu (2023) and Onikoyi et al. (2023) explored the use of Naïve Bayes and 
other classification algorithms in analyzing linguistic patterns, demonstrating their 
reliability and interpretability in automated text evaluation. 
Project Context 
 
Many academic papers demonstrate low or inconsistent Gender and 
Development (GAD) responsiveness, resulting in research outputs that inadequately 
address inclusivity, equality, and gender sensitivity. Despite existing guidelines, a 
significant number of studies fail to integrate GAD principles effectively, leading to 
insufficiently gender-responsive research from Palma (2025) and Sicuan (2024). 
Guided by the principles of the Harmonized Gender and Development Guidelines 
 
 
(HGDG), this study recognizes the need for a systematic, technology-driven approach 
that can help identify and address gaps in GAD integration, ultimately supporting the 
production of more comprehensive and responsive academic studies. 
Objectives of the Study 
         The general objective of the study is to develop an automated assessment 
system that evaluates academic articles based on the Harmonized Gender and 
Development Guidelines (HGDG) checklist and Naïve Bayes Algorithm.  
Specifically, it aimed to: 
1. design and implement a model that: 
a. automatically assess academic articles based on the key aspects: 
(1) Gender-Sensitive Language, 
(2) Representation of Women and Men, 
(3) Inclusivity in Language, and 
(4) Authorship and Citations; 
b. classifies textual content using the Naive Bayes algorithm and analyzes 
contextual meaning using the BERT model; 
c. generates evaluation results and corresponding recommendations; and 
d. provides necessary reports such as assessment summaries. 
2. develop the web system prototype that: (1) allows file upload (PyMuPDF and 
python-docx), preprocesses text (NLTK), performs feature (TF-IDF), performs 
GAD assessment of academic papers (Naïve Bayes and BERT), and displays 
results in tabular form (2) uses React.js to provide a responsive and user-
friendly web interface (3) use Python (Flask) for backend processing, and (4) 
use Firebase storing user data, files, and results; 
3. test the system in terms of model and functional testing; and 
4. evaluate the system using the adapted ISO-IEC 25010 software and data 
quality evaluation instrument. 
 
 
Purpose and Description 
The primary purpose of this study is to address the inadequate and inconsistent 
Gender and Development (GAD) responsiveness in academic papers. Many research 
outputs fail to fully integrate gender-sensitive language, fair representation of women 
and men, inclusivity, and proper citation practices, resulting in studies that are not fully 
gender-responsive. 
This project aims to provide a systematic and technology-assisted approach to 
support more comprehensive and inclusive academic writing by identifying gaps in 
Gender and Development (GAD) responsiveness and offering actionable insights for 
improvement. In doing so, it benefits authors by guiding them in enhancing the gender 
sensitivity, inclusivity, and fairness of their manuscripts, helps reviewers and editors 
maintain consistent and objective evaluation standards across a wide range of 
submissions, and assists academic institutions in upholding, reinforcing, and further 
developing their commitment to gender-responsive research. By integrating automated 
assessment tools with established GAD principles, the system promotes not only 
improved quality of individual research outputs but also contributes to the goal of 
fostering a scholarly environment and ultimately, a society that values equity, 
inclusivity, and the active implementation of gender-responsive perspectives in 
creating academic papers. 
Time and Place of the Study 
The study will be carried out from July 2025 to July 2026 at the Cavite State 
University - CCAT Campus, covering all phases from the preparation of the project 
outline to the completion of the final manuscript. 
Scope and Limitation of the Study 
The primary purpose of this study is to develop an automated web-based 
system that evaluates the Gender and Development (GAD) responsiveness of 
 
 
academic papers. The system utilizes text classification, specifically the Naïve Bayes 
Classifier and integrates the Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers 
(BERT) model to enhance contextual understanding. The main functions of this system 
include text preprocessing, feature extraction, Naïve Bayes and BERT classification, 
report generation, and user authentication. The system will also provide features for 
uploading research papers, viewing responsiveness results, managing user accounts, 
and accessing assessment summaries for monitoring and evaluation.  
For the limitations, this study does not cover the full range of GAD assessment 
indicators other than the four selected concepts. This includes other dimensions of 
gender and development evaluation such as participation and decision-making, 
capacity development, benefits and opportunities for women and men, monitoring and 
evaluation mechanisms, and sustainability of gender mainstreaming efforts. 
Additionally, the system does not assess institutional policies, budget allocation for 
GAD programs, or the broader socio-political context influencing gender 
responsiveness in research. The focus of this study remains limited to text-based 
analysis within academic manuscripts, excluding non-textual elements such as 
images, tables, and reference formatting that may also convey gender bias or 
imbalance. Moreover, while the system generates automated preliminary assessments 
and recommendations, final evaluations and decisions remain under the discretion of 
human evaluators to ensure fairness, accuracy, and comprehensive understanding.

Definition of Terms 
 
The following terms are operationally defined in the study: 
Academic Paper refers to a written research output, such as a thesis, journal article, 
or report, that presents findings and analysis on a specific topic within an academic 
context. 
Gender Bias – refers to the preferential or prejudiced treatment of individuals based 
on gender, which can influence decision-making, authorship recognition, and citation 
patterns in academic writing. 
Gender-Sensitive Language – refers to a form of communication that avoids bias, 
stereotypes, or discrimination based on gender. 
Gender and Development (GAD) – refers to a framework that promotes equality and 
inclusivity between women and men by integrating gender perspectives in research, 
policies, and development programs. 
GAD Responsiveness – refers to the extent to which a research paper or project 
actively responds to and promotes gender sensitivity, equality, and inclusivity 
principles in alignment with the GAD framework. 
Harmonized Gender and Development Guidelines (HGDG) – refers to a set of 
standardized tools and concepts developed to assess the gender-responsiveness of 
programs, projects, and research outputs. 
Inclusive Language – refers to a language that promotes equality and respect for all 
individuals by avoiding assumptions or stereotypes related to gender, race, or other 
social categories.  
 
 
Machine Learning (ML) – refers to a subset of Artificial Intelligence that uses 
algorithms to learn patterns from data. It can be applied to detect and correct biased 
language automatically in academic papers.  
Natural Language Processing (NLP) – refers to a field of artificial intelligence 
focused on enabling computers to understand, interpret, and process human language 
in text form. 
Naïve Bayes Algorithm – refers to a probabilistic machine learning algorithm based 
on Bayes’ Theorem, used for text classification by predicting the likelihood that a 
document belongs to a specific category. 
BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) – refers to a 
deep learning model that analyzes text bidirectionally to understand word context and 
meaning for tasks like classification and contextual analysis. 
 
 
 
 
REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE 
This chapter presents the body of existing research and literature relevant to 
the study, “Automated Assessment of Academic Articles for GAD Responsiveness 
using Naïve Bayes and BERT.” It explores previous works, frameworks, and 
technological advancements that form the foundation of the present research. 
The growing integration of technology in gender analysis and inclusivity 
evaluation has led to a surge in studies focused on detecting gender bias, promoting 
fairness, and automating responsiveness frameworks. Recent work by Mousavian et 
al. (2025) highlighted how large language models (LLMs) can be leveraged to measure 
and mitigate gender bias within digital systems, emphasizing transparency and ethical 
data practices. Similarly, Llorens et al. (2021) found that gender bias in academic 
writing not only reflects but also reinforces social inequalities, influencing the career 
progression and well-being of underrepresented groups. These findings show that the 
problem of bias persists across both human-written and machine-generated texts. 
Collectively, these studies parallel the need for frameworks such as the Gender and 
Development (GAD) programs that ensure inclusiveness in institutional and academic 
outputs. Together, they demonstrate that both computational and institutional 
approaches are converging toward one goal: the fair and unbiased treatment of gender 
representation in digital and organizational contexts. 
Gender equality and inclusivity remain foundational principles in evaluating 
development programs and academic work. The Harmonized Gender and 
Development Guidelines (HGDG), established by the Philippine Commission on 
Women (PCW, 2020), provide a comprehensive framework for assessing gender 
responsiveness 
in 
planning, 
implementation, 
and 
evaluation 
processes. 
Complementing this, the UN Women (2020) rapid assessment tool and its Gender-
Responsive Evaluation Handbook (UN Women, 2022) establish systematic ways to 
 
 
assess equality outcomes across various contexts, highlighting how gender sensitivity 
can be measured through evidence-based indicators. Similarly, the Commission on 
Audit (COA, 2020) GAD Audit Guide offers institutional auditing guidelines, while the 
DBM/PEFA (2024–2025) report focuses on integrating gender-responsive public 
financial management to ensure inclusivity in budgeting and resource allocation. 
These frameworks collectively stress accountability and measurable equity in both 
global and local settings. 
Complementing these institutional tools, the Philippine Commission on Women 
(PCW, 2025) issued the Gender Mainstreaming Evaluation Framework (GMEF), 
emphasizing self-assessment as a key mechanism for continuous improvement in 
gender inclusivity programs. However, while UN Women focuses on humanitarian and 
global programmatic approaches, local instruments like COA’s GAD Audit and PCW’s 
GMEF emphasize bureaucratic and institutional compliance—revealing a divergence 
between international and domestic applications of gender evaluation (UN Women, 
2022; PCW, 2025). These converging yet distinct approaches highlight a key 
challenge: translating policy-level inclusivity frameworks into consistent evaluative and 
computational methods suitable for automated systems. 
In the Philippine higher education context, Palma (2024) emphasized that 
although many institutions have adopted GAD programs, awareness among faculty 
and students regarding gender-related laws and policies remains limited. Similarly, 
Sicuan (2024) reported that while HGDG implementation improves gender awareness 
and participation in education, significant gaps persist in training and resource 
allocation. Ray et al. (2022) and Brück et al. (2023) revealed that citation and 
authorship imbalances remain prevalent, mirroring larger structural inequities that also 
appear in academic funding and publication. Lee et al. (2023) further showed that 
gender productivity gaps widened during the COVID-19 pandemic, amplifying these 
disparities. These studies collectively demonstrate that institutional policies alone are 
 
 
insufficient, and that automated tools could augment human evaluation by identifying 
linguistic and structural bias patterns in academic writing. 
Negative findings also highlight ongoing gender unresponsiveness in various 
domains. Trinkenreich et al. (2022) examined challenges faced by women in the 
software industry and found persistent underrepresentation and lack of inclusive 
policies that restrict women’s participation in technical fields. Similarly, Sagcal and 
Ramos (2024) investigated GAD program implementation in selected barangays in 
Nueva Ecija and revealed weak institutionalization and insufficient funding for gender 
initiatives. These gaps justify the development of automated GAD assessment 
systems that can complement human oversight, streamline compliance evaluation, 
and address unresponsiveness in both local and professional contexts. 
Recent research underscores that gender-sensitive wording plays a crucial role 
in shaping inclusivity, perception, and equality in both communication and institutional 
discourse. Hetjens et al. (2024) demonstrated that job listings written with gender-fair 
language significantly increased engagement and reduced perceived bias, revealing 
how linguistic framing can shape audience response and participation. Similarly, 
Kricheli-Katz and Regev (2021) found that grammatical gender markers unconsciously 
influence perceptions of competence, resulting in measurable disadvantages for 
women in professional and academic contexts. These findings converge on the idea 
that linguistic form carries implicit bias, while diverging in their methodologies, Hetjens 
et al. focusing on behavioral outcomes, and Kricheli-Katz and Regev exploring 
linguistic structure. Such studies underscore the necessity of automating the detection 
and correction of gendered expressions, which manual audits often overlook due to 
scale and subjectivity. 
Beyond individual wording, deeper representational issues persist in the 
portrayal and visibility of women and men in various fields. Fruehwirth (2024) revealed 
 
 
that occupational and academic narratives continue to favor men through the use of 
agentic descriptors such as “leader” or “innovator,” while women are often described 
with communal or supportive language. Goyanes (2024) further demonstrated that 
female scholars receive disproportionately fewer citations and lower h-index scores, 
reinforcing systemic underrepresentation. While these studies differ in focus—one 
linguistic, one bibliometric—they converge in highlighting that both textual description 
and citation practice contribute to structural inequity. Addressing these disparities 
requires systems capable of analyzing both the surface language and deeper 
contextual structures—a dual capability achievable through models such as Naïve 
Bayes for lexical detection and BERT for semantic contextualization. 
Inclusivity in language extends beyond gender representation alone, 
encompassing pronoun use, occupational terminology, and exclusionary expressions 
embedded in everyday writing. Doughman et al. (2021) categorized multiple 
dimensions of gender bias, including occupational stereotypes, gendered pronouns, 
and implicit exclusion, and created annotated corpora that enable supervised learning 
models to detect such patterns. Stańczak and Augenstein (2021) expanded this 
analysis, showing that NLP models themselves can perpetuate discrimination through 
binary gender assumptions embedded in training data. Their works converge in 
exposing algorithmic bias as a byproduct of unbalanced datasets, yet diverge in their 
solutions—Doughman et al. promote data curation and labeling, while Stańczak and 
Augenstein call for architecture-level reform. Together, these studies affirm the 
relevance of fairness-aware models like Naïve Bayes and BERT in advancing 
automated GAD evaluation tools capable of identifying inclusivity gaps at both 
linguistic and algorithmic levels. 
Even in the sphere of authorship and citation, gender imbalance remains 
pervasive. Dworkin et al. (2020) identified systematic under-citation of women across 
scientific literature, a pattern that limits their academic visibility and perpetuates 
 
 
professional disparities. Wu (2024) corroborated these findings, observing that citation 
networks consistently favor male scholars, even when research quality is comparable. 
These converging results reveal how authorship and citation patterns mirror the same 
biases that appear in language and representation—implying that inclusivity must also 
extend to credit attribution. Automating the analysis of these patterns through 
computational tools enables more transparent and data-driven assessments of gender 
equity within research ecosystems. 
To operationalize gender analysis principles, numerous computational and 
algorithmic innovations have emerged in recent years, bridging linguistic fairness with 
artificial intelligence. Vanmassenhove, Emmery, and Shterionov (2021) introduced the 
NeuTral Rewriter, a multilingual Neural Machine Translation (NMT) system built on 
Transformer architectures, designed to automatically transform gendered English, 
Spanish, and German sentences into gender-neutral equivalents without losing 
meaning. The model, fine-tuned with a custom dataset emphasizing gendered text, 
applied attention mechanisms to guide word substitutions that maintained contextual 
integrity. Testing involved evaluating BLEU and gender accuracy scores across 
multiple corpora, and results showed BLEU scores between 27–34 and a 25–40% 
reduction in gender bias, highlighting the model’s effectiveness in generating fluent, 
gender-neutral text. Similarly, Alhafni et al. (2022) conducted a multilingual shared task 
on gender rewriting using Transformer-based sequence-to-sequence models, notably 
ArabicT5, which integrated word-level gender detection for morphologically rich 
languages. The models generated multiple gender-rewritten alternatives for each 
sentence to align with gendered person references, and testing on a blind set of 7,318 
sentences achieved 98% inter-annotator agreement and 96.2% exact match accuracy. 
Results indicated strong rewriting performance, with top systems attaining an M2 F0.5 
of approximately 88.42, proving the reliability of contextual gender rewriting. 
 
 
Further developments in modeling semantic fairness stemmed from 
transformer-based analyses of bias in contextual embeddings. Jentzsch and Turan 
(2022) employed BERT and its variants to measure latent gender bias by analyzing 
sentiment shifts between male and female textual variations. Through sentiment 
analysis tasks across pretrained and fine-tuned BERT models, their testing confirmed 
consistent polarity differences that traced bias back to pretraining corpora, and results 
highlighted measurable sentiment asymmetry, proving BERT’s tendency to encode 
gender bias. Complementing this, Dusi et al. (2022) utilized BERT contextual 
embeddings enhanced by weakly supervised feature selection and dimensionality 
reduction visualization to map gendered occupational terms in vector space. Their 
method enabled visual interpretation of gender association clusters, and testing 
correlated embedding distributions with real-world occupational data, revealing a 
strong alignment between computational and social gender imbalances—evidence 
that contextual models mirror real-world disparities. 
Efforts toward interpretability in gender bias detection continued through the 
work of Butt et al. (2021), who fine-tuned BERT and RoBERTa for detecting sexist, 
exclusionary, and stereotype-driven expressions in social and formal text. Their 
methodology leveraged attention-based interpretability to link bias predictions to 
specific tokens. Through evaluation on annotated social media and news corpora, their 
testing achieved F1 scores between 0.81–0.84, and results showed that attention 
maps effectively localized biased expressions, enhancing explainability. Expanding 
this approach, Das et al. (2023) designed explainable transformer pipelines built upon 
BERT architectures, integrating rationale generation to justify bias classifications. The 
models produced natural-language explanations for flagged bias, and testing across 
multiple benchmark datasets yielded 82% accuracy, with human evaluators verifying 
that explanations were coherent and trustworthy, underscoring the role of transparency 
in NLP fairness systems. 
 
 
Beyond textual semantics, Ioannidis et al. (2023) and Kong et al. (2022) 
explored gender disparity in academia using bibliometric automation. Ioannidis et al. 
utilized automated author-gender inference algorithms combined with bibliometric 
network analysis to examine representation among over 100,000 publications. The 
method inferred author genders and analyzed citation fairness, with testing validated 
against known author datasets, revealing persistent underrepresentation of women in 
high-impact networks. Similarly, Kong et al. (2022) applied graph-based algorithms to 
co-authorship and citation networks, quantifying collaboration patterns and first-mover 
advantages. Testing on large-scale bibliometric datasets showed systemic disparities 
in academic visibility, and results confirmed how network structures reinforce gendered 
recognition gaps—concepts foundational to citation fairness evaluation in the 
proposed GAD responsiveness system. 
At the intersection of linguistic fairness and automated ethics, Sobhani et al. 
(2024) used context-aware ranking models to assess gender balance in large 
language model (LLM) outputs. Their method compared text generations across 
prompts to measure subtle gender imbalance, while testing involved multi-scenario 
evaluation of ranking fairness. Results demonstrated the model’s capacity to identify 
fine-grained biases using fairness scores rather than classification metrics. Building on 
this, Kumar et al. (2024) developed the LLM-as-Judge pipeline, which employed 
advanced LLMs such as GPT-4 and Claude as automated fairness auditors. The 
approach applied sociolinguistic heuristics to unseen text, evaluating inclusivity with 
context sensitivity. Testing across various datasets compared model evaluations to 
human fairness judgments, achieving over 91% alignment accuracy. Results 
confirmed that large-scale LLMs could replicate human-like fairness reasoning, 
providing a scalable model for gender responsiveness auditing. 
Complementing these modern systems, Bartl et al. (2020) investigated 
Transformer embeddings (BERT) augmented with counterfactual data augmentation 
 
 
to reduce gender bias in NLP outputs. Their approach generated gender-swapped text 
to assess bias resilience, and testing on augmented datasets showed 15–20% 
reductions in bias metrics. Results validated the method’s effectiveness in mitigating 
bias while preserving performance. Similarly, De Vassimon Manela et al. (2021) 
employed adversarial debiasing within BERT embeddings to mitigate gender 
stereotyping during sentence classification tasks. Testing on benchmark datasets 
revealed a 10–15% decrease in bias scores without loss of accuracy, demonstrating 
that embedding-level mitigation can yield balanced representations. Meanwhile, Brown 
et al. (2020) introduced GPT-3, a 175-billion parameter autoregressive transformer, 
highlighting its unprecedented natural language generation capabilities. Testing 
through few-shot learning tasks revealed high fluency but also embedded gender and 
racial biases. Results underscored the duality of power and risk in large-scale models, 
emphasizing the need for ethical constraints. Extending the scope, Hobbs (2025) 
employed transformer-based evaluation frameworks to analyze inclusivity in terms of 
gender and sexual identity. The study benchmarked outputs against annotated 
datasets representing intersectional identities, with results exposing persistent gaps in 
LLM fairness across marginalized demographics, urging more intersectionally-aware 
AI evaluations. 
In statistical and probabilistic modeling, Naïve Bayes continues to serve as a 
robust and interpretable classifier for linguistic bias detection. Angeles et al. (2021) 
and Tiffani (2020) demonstrated its efficiency in detecting gendered linguistic features 
and sentiment polarity. Both studies used text preprocessing steps—tokenization and 
stemming—before training the model on labeled corpora. Testing achieved 
classification accuracies of 78–82% and ~80%, respectively, validating Naïve Bayes’ 
reliability in gendered language identification. Expanding the comparison, Gupte et al. 
(2021) evaluated both probabilistic (Naïve Bayes) and neural (LSTM) models on 
gendered datasets. The study found neural models achieved higher precision (~85%), 
 
 
but Naïve Bayes maintained better interpretability with ~81% accuracy. Similarly, Liu 
(2023) introduced hybrid probabilistic-neural classifiers that merged Naïve Bayes and 
neural networks for multilingual text classification. Testing across languages produced 
~79% accuracy for Naïve Bayes and ~83% for hybrid models, reflecting the 
adaptability of probabilistic methods in cross-lingual bias analysis. Extending this 
further, Wu (2025) optimized Naïve Bayes with dataset rebalancing, applying it to 
multilingual corpora to address skewed gender distributions. Results showed a 12% 
reduction in false negatives and overall accuracy of 81%, enhancing fairness 
detection. Onikoyi (2023) applied Naïve Bayes for gender inference, predicting 
authors’ gender orientations based on pronoun and linguistic cues. Testing on 
manually annotated datasets achieved ~80% accuracy, reaffirming Naïve Bayes as an 
efficient baseline for gender pattern detection. 
Several practical applications have also emerged, demonstrating the 
integration of fairness analytics in real-world systems. As previously mentioned, the 
NeuTral Rewriter developed by Vanmassenhove et al. (2021) is one of the earliest and 
most influential systems aimed at automatic gender-neutral rewriting. It combines rule-
based grammatical transformations with neural machine translation (NMT) to identify 
gendered expressions and rewrite them into neutral forms without compromising 
semantic accuracy. For example, it converts sentences such as “The chairman opened 
the meeting” to “The chairperson opened the meeting.” This hybrid approach 
effectively integrates symbolic linguistic knowledge with deep learning flexibility, which 
mirrors the planned design of the current system that merges probabilistic models 
(Naïve Bayes) and contextual embeddings (BERT). The NeuTral Rewriter’s 
interpretability, achieved through token-level alignment and replacement explanation, 
also serves as a methodological inspiration for the interpretive interface of the GAD 
system, ensuring that users can trace why a text was flagged as gender-biased. 
Sumner (2020) developed the Gender Balance Assessment Tool (GBAT), combining 
 
 
name-gender inference algorithms and database lookups to compute gender 
proportions in bibliographies, course syllabi, and citation lists. Testing on real 
academic datasets verified high accuracy in detecting imbalanced citation practices, 
and results showed consistent underrepresentation of female authors. Meanwhile, 
Göritz, Stattkus, and Beinke (2022) introduced a Bias-Detection Dashboard utilizing 
keyword co-occurrence matrices, sentiment scoring, and heatmap visualization to 
represent bias patterns in textual corpora. The system, validated against human-
annotated corpora, achieved ~90% agreement, offering interpretable visual analytics 
for gender bias detection. Extending this visualization-driven approach, Kumar et al. 
(2024) emphasized LLM-based interpretive fairness, proposing an interactive auditing 
paradigm adaptable for responsive systems. Finally, Dworkin et al. (2020) presented 
the Gender Citation Balance Index (GCBI), which applied automated name extraction 
and gender inference to quantify disparities in citation networks. Testing across over 
1,000 journals revealed that women authors were cited 20–30% less than their male 
counterparts, with results confirming systemic citation bias. 
In essence, these related systems not only provide theoretical and technical 
precedents but also serve as modular inspirations for building a comprehensive, 
automated GAD evaluation platform. The integration of text-level rewriting (from 
NeuTral Rewriter), gender-balance metrics (from GBAT and GCBI), visualization and 
interpretability (from Göritz et al.), and scalable fairness assessment (from Kumar et 
al.) collectively defines a path toward a multi-dimensional system that addresses both 
the linguistic and structural aspects of gender bias. Such convergence exemplifies how 
computational intelligence can operationalize inclusivity principles outlined by the GAD 
responsiveness criteria. 
From institutional frameworks to computational systems, research increasingly 
aligns toward achieving measurable gender fairness and inclusivity in language, 
representation, and authorship. Different studies approach this goal from varying 
 
 
directions—some emphasizing policy implementation and social awareness, others 
focusing on automation and algorithmic precision. Together, these perspectives reveal 
that genuine gender responsiveness relies on both human insight and technological 
innovation. Building on this progression, the proposed system integrates machine 
learning methods such as Naïve Bayes and BERT to evaluate academic papers 
through the lens of GAD principles, aiming to transform traditional manual 
assessments into a transparent, data-driven process that promotes equality in 
academic discourse.

The Automated Assessment of Academic Papers’ GAD Responsiveness with 
Naïve Bayes and BERT is a web-based system that automatically evaluates the 
gender responsiveness of academic papers based on four key aspects: (1) Gender-
Sensitive Language, (2) Representation of Women and Men, (3) Inclusivity in 
Language, and (4) Authorship and Citations. It combines natural language processing 
(NLP) and machine learning techniques to detect non-GAD-responsive content and 
provide corrective suggestions for improvement. The system draws from research in 
explainable AI and fairness-aware language models (Mousavian et al., 2025; Sobhani 
et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024), aligning computational tools with the broader goal of 
promoting inclusivity in academic writing. 
 
 
 
 
Gender and Development Framework Integration 
The conceptual foundation of the system draws inspiration from institutional 
gender evaluation frameworks, such as the Philippine Commission on Women’s 
Harmonized Gender and Development Guidelines (HGDG) and the Gender 
Mainstreaming Evaluation Framework (GMEF). While the system does not directly 
apply these frameworks, it takes guidance from their emphasis on inclusivity, equity, 
and measurable responsiveness in content evaluation. Similarly, global tools such as 
the UN Women (2020, 2022) Gender-Responsive Evaluation Handbook and the 
Commission on Audit’s (2020) GAD Audit Guide inform the system’s goal of 
standardizing fair, objective assessment practices. By integrating these principles into 
automated evaluation, the system transforms subjective GAD checking into a more 
consistent and data-driven process, supporting institutional efforts toward gender 
fairness in academic writing. 
Web-Based Architecture 
The system is designed as a web platform developed using React.js, Flask, 
Firebase, and NLTK. React.js is used to create an interactive user interface where 
users can upload academic papers and view analysis results. Flask, a Python-based 
framework, manages backend processes, including NLP preprocessing, model 
execution, and classification. Firebase serves as the cloud-based database for storing 
processed data and results, ensuring scalability and accessibility. 
Following the design principles of web-based analytical systems proposed by 
Göritz et al. (2022) and Kumar et al. (2024), the integration of these technologies 
allows the system to perform real-time gender-responsiveness assessments efficiently 
while maintaining a user-friendly and secure web environment. This architecture 
enables seamless interaction between the analytical components and the user 
interface, ensuring that automated evaluations are both accessible and interpretable 
to users. 
 
 
Natural Language Processing and Preprocessing 
The preprocessing stage prepares uploaded text for analysis using the Natural 
Language Toolkit (NLTK). This includes tokenization, stop-word removal, and 
stemming—steps proven to enhance the accuracy of text-based classification 
(Angeles et al., 2021; Tiffani, 2020). During this stage, gendered words, pronouns, and 
exclusionary terms are normalized to allow consistent analysis by the models. 
Following the approach of Doughman et al. (2021), the preprocessing also ensures 
that linguistic cues such as role descriptors and gendered associations are properly 
captured before being evaluated by the Naïve Bayes and BERT classifiers. 
Naïve Bayes Classifier (NBC) 
Naïve Bayes is a probabilistic text classification model based on Bayes’ 
theorem, which assumes independence among features. Angeles et al. (2021) and 
Tiffani (2020) applied Naïve Bayes to detect gendered linguistic patterns in academic 
and social texts. The model calculates the likelihood of a text belonging to a gendered 
or neutral category based on word frequencies and conditional probabilities. In testing, 
Naïve Bayes achieved classification accuracies ranging from 78% to 82%, 
demonstrating efficient identification of gendered expressions. Wu (2025) further 
improved its performance by tuning the model and rebalancing skewed datasets, 
reducing false negatives by approximately 12%. Naïve Bayes is well-suited for 
automated GAD responsiveness systems due to its computational efficiency and 
interpretability, particularly for lexical-level bias detection and authorship analysis. 
Naïve Bayes Algorithm  
The Naïve Bayes algorithm functions as one of the core classifiers of the 
system. Known for its simplicity, interpretability, and effectiveness in text classification 
(Gupte et al., 2021; Liu, 2023), Naïve Bayes detects surface-level linguistic patterns, 
such as biased word choices or gender-exclusive terms. In this system, it is applied to 
assess multiple criteria, particularly Criterion 1, 3, and 4, authorship and citations, as 
 
 
well as to assist in the identification of gendered or stereotypical phrases. By analyzing 
word frequency and probability distributions, Naïve Bayes categorizes sentences or 
phrases as either GAD-compliant or non-GAD-compliant, providing fast and reliable 
results suitable for real-time feedback. 
BERT Model 
Complementing Naïve Bayes, the system employs BERT (Bidirectional 
Encoder Representations from Transformers) for deeper, context-based analysis. As 
shown in the studies of Dusi et al. (2022) and Butt et al. (2021), BERT effectively 
captures the relationships between words, enabling detection of subtle biases that 
occur in sentence structure and meaning. In the system, BERT is specifically used for 
Criterion 2: Representation of Women and Men, where contextual understanding is 
essential. For instance, BERT can detect implicit stereotypes or biased portrayals even 
if gendered terms are not explicitly mentioned. Its contextual embeddings enhance the 
interpretability and fairness of the system’s overall analysis. 
Hybrid Model Integration 
The system adopts a hybrid architecture combining Naïve Bayes and BERT, 
inspired by Vanmassenhove et al. (2021) and Alhafni et al. (2022). This dual-model 
structure leverages the strengths of both approaches—Naïve Bayes for surface-level 
lexical bias detection and BERT for deeper contextual understanding. The combination 
improves the system’s precision and recall across all four GAD criteria, ensuring that 
both explicit and implicit forms of gender bias are identified. This integration allows the 
system to provide both statistical and semantic perspectives in evaluating gender 
responsiveness. 
Neural Machine Translation-Based Gender Rewriting (NeuTral Rewriter) 
Vanmassenhove, Emmery, and Shterionov (2021) developed the NeuTral 
Rewriter, a multilingual Transformer-based NMT system designed to rewrite sentences 
 
 
into gender-neutral forms. The model was fine-tuned on a custom dataset containing 
gendered texts in English, Spanish, and German, using attention mechanisms to guide 
word choices toward neutrality. Evaluations on parallel corpora employed BLEU and 
gender accuracy scores, achieving BLEU 27–34 and a 25–40% reduction in gender 
bias. The system preserved grammatical and contextual integrity while neutralizing 
biased phrasing. In the proposed GAD system, NeuTral Rewriter’s approach informs 
a text-rewriting module that suggests inclusive alternatives to gendered sentences. 
Large Language Models (LLMs) / GPT Approaches 
Large Language Models such as GPT-3 and GPT-4 are autoregressive 
transformers capable of generating high-quality text and performing complex natural 
language tasks. Brown et al. (2020) demonstrated GPT-3’s potential and risks, 
including the propagation of bias in generated outputs. Kumar et al. (2024) introduced 
the LLM-as-Judge pipeline, where GPT-4 and Claude models acted as fairness 
auditors by applying sociolinguistic heuristics to unseen texts. Sobhani et al. (2024) 
employed context-aware ranking models to measure fairness in LLM outputs, while 
Hobbs (2025) emphasized intersectional representation. These approaches enable 
adaptive evaluation of gender inclusivity, allowing the system to score text fairness 
dynamically and provide rationales for detected biases. 
Ethical and Fairness Considerations 
The system adheres to the ethical AI principles discussed by Mousavian et al. 
(2025) and Stańczak and Augenstein (2021), emphasizing transparency, fairness, and 
bias mitigation in data and model design. It employs fairness-aware preprocessing and 
context-based reweighting to reduce potential algorithmic bias during training. These 
measures align with the recommendations of Sobhani et al. (2024) and Hobbs (2025), 
who underscored the necessity of contextual fairness and intersectional sensitivity in 
NLP systems. Through these strategies, the proposed system not only automates 
 
 
evaluation but also ensures that its methods remain consistent with ethical standards 
in AI and gender studies.

Despite these advancements, a significant gap remains in systematically 
evaluating gender responsiveness in academic writing. Many scholarly articles 
continue to exhibit gendered language, representational imbalance, and citation

solution that enhances equity and inclusiveness in academic publications.

to capture contextual meaning more accurately, including subtle cues and implied 
semantics.

academic articles for Gender and Development (GAD) responsiveness through text 
extraction, linguistic analysis, and classification using the Naive Bayes algorithm. 
Specifically: 
a) The document upload and extraction component receives academic articles 
in supported file formats and converts their content into plain, analyzable text. 
b) The text preprocessing module refines the extracted text by removing stop 
words, punctuation, and irrelevant characters, retaining only essential 
linguistic elements. 
c) The feature extraction process detects linguistic features such as gendered 
terms, pronouns, and inclusivity indicators that contribute to GAD 
responsiveness assessment. 
d) The Naive Bayes classification module evaluates the extracted features and 
determines whether each academic article is categorized as “Responsive” or 
“Non-Responsive.” 
e) The report generation and visualization module produces a clear, structured 
summary of the classification results along with relevant recommendations. 
Model Testing 
Model Testing evaluates the performance and reliability of the trained 
classification models used in the system. It ensures that both the Naive Bayes and 
BERT models accurately identify GAD-responsive and non-responsive content based 
on predefined key aspects.  
 
 
Confusion Matrix 
The confusion matrix is a matrix of numbers that tell us where a model gets 
confused. It is a class-wise distribution of the predictive performance of a 
classification model, that is, the confusion matrix is an organized way of mapping the 
predictions to the original classes to which the data belong (Kundu R. 2022). 
The confusion matrix is a performance evaluation tool that summarizes the 
results of a classification model. It shows how many predictions were correct or 
incorrect by comparing the model’s outputs with the actual labels. 
Elements of the Confusion Matrix: 
● True Positive (TP): Instances where the model correctly predicts a positive 
class. 
● True Negative (TN): Instances where the model correctly predicts a negative 
class. 
● False Positive (FP): Instances where the model incorrectly predicts a 
positive class when it is actually negative. 
● False Negative (FN): Instances where the model incorrectly predicts a 
negative class when it is actually positive. 
From this matrix, performance metrics are calculated: 
● Accuracy: Overall correctness of predictions.
